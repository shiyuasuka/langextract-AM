## A B S T R A C T

Metal additive manufacturing (AM) produces unique grain morphologies owing to the high cooling rates and large temperature gradients, which potentially lead to unexpected mechanical anisotropy. In this study, we unveil an anomalous anisotropic behaviour in a solid-solution-strengthened superalloy with periodic columnar-to-crescent grains fabricated by laser powder bed fusion (LPBF). Specifically, as-built (AB) specimens show higher strength perpendicular to the build direction (BD) than that parallel to the BD at room temperature (RT), while the opposite trend occurs at the elevated temperature (ET, 900 °C). Besides, the heat treatment eliminates the anisotropy of strength at both RT and ET. A dislocation-based damage-coupled crystal plasticity finite element (CPFE) model with strain gradients is utilized to understand the origin of the above anomalous anisotropy. It is found that the transition of anisotropy from RT to ET is attributed to the temperature-dependent dislocation annihilation combined with initial dislocations in AB state. In contrast to the heat-treated specimens without anisotropy, the LPBF-induced residual deformation primarily contributes to the anisotropy at RT, whereas the initial dislocations dominate the anomalous anisotropy at ETs for AB specimens. The CPFE model reveals the threshold temperature to be 600 °C for the occurrence of anomalous anisotropy, which is experimentally validated. This study presents a comprehensive understanding into temperature-dependent anisotropy of AM superalloys, and in turn guides the regulation of anisotropy by tuning microstructures.

### 1. Introduction

Additive manufacturing (AM) of superalloys is promising to achieve fast, near-net shaping of hot components in aerospace applications (Dang et al., 2025; Gu et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2024b). Laser powder bed fusion (LPBF) is one of the most precise AM technologies, in which a focused laser beam is used to selectively melt a powder bed in a layer-by-layer sequence to build the components (Blakey-Milner et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2025). Recently, LPBF technique has been applied to fabricate the superalloy fuel nozzles and many other critical parts (Sun et al., 2021). Compared to conventionally manufactured counterparts, LPBF-fabricated nozzles have been demonstrated to exhibit reduced weight and enhanced durability, as reported in several studies (NASA, 2023; Subeshan et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2021). Moreover, LPBF technique shows potentials to impart superior mechanical properties for superalloy components, and offers tunable strength and ductility through various heat treatment strategies (Lu et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024). Therefore, such advances demonstrate the potential of LPBF technology in fabricating high-performance superalloy components for critical applications.

Under high cooling rates ( $ >2 \times 10^5 $ K/s) and large temperature gradients ( $ >5 \times 10^5 $ K/m) (Okugawa et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2021), some unique LPBF-induced microstructures in superalloys possibly lead to anisotropic mechanical behaviour. A substantial number of studies conducted at room temperature (RT) have found that, the LPBF Inconel 718 superalloy exhibited higher strength in the horizontal direction (perpendicular to the build direction (BD)) compared with the vertical direction (parallel to BD) (Cui et al., 2023). For instance, Chen et al. (Chen et al., 2018) reported that the yield strength (YS) under horizontal tension was 23 MPa higher than that under vertical tension in a LPBF K418 superalloy. Similarly, Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2023a) demonstrated that horizontal tension exhibited a YS that is 101 MPa higher than that of vertical tension for a LPBF GH4099 superalloy. In those studies, numerous columnar grains parallel to the BD with strong anisotropy, were formed driven by the temperature gradient in the LPBF process, which was summarized as the key reason for the higher YS perpendicular to BD. Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2021) also demonstrated that the HX superalloys fabricated under different laser scanning strategies (0°, 90° and 67° hatch angles and a chessboard pattern with a 67° hatch angle) shared a similar anisotropy behaviour at RT. Therefore, the higher YS perpendicular to BD has been widely recognized as a common anisotropic behaviour in previous studies at RT, while it remains unknown whether this trend persists at elevated temperatures (ETs).

Since superalloys are designed for high-temperature applications exceeding 600 °C, it is essential to focus on the mechanical properties of LPBF superalloys at ETs (Hu et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). Unlike the widely recognized anisotropy trend at RT, no clear consensus regarding the anisotropic behaviour at ETs has been established for LPBF superalloys (Farias et al., 2024; Rezaei et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2020). For example, Yu et al. printed an HX superalloy with strong textures and high initial

Farias et al. reported a similar anisotropic behaviour for an AM superalloy with only large columnar grains at 1050 °C (Farias et al., 2024). In contrast, Rezaei et al. reported that an LPBF IN718 superalloy with the transition of columnar-to-crescent grains, exhibited higher strength under the horizontal tension at RT, whereas the load-bearing capacity was larger under vertical tension at 650 °C (Rezaei et al., 2021). Those inconsistent results arise from the AM-induced intricate microstructures such as grain morphology, initial dislocations, and residual stresses (Wang et al., 2023). Their effects on macroscopic anisotropy at ETs have not been comprehensively understood.

The anisotropic behaviour of LPBF superalloys is attributed to several AM-induced factors (Bean et al., 2019; Rezaei et al., 2020). Previous studies have identified some potential contributors to the anisotropy, including crystallographic texture, orientation of the columnar grains, residual stresses formed in the LPBF process, and high initial dislocation densities (Kok et al., 2018; Rezaei et al., 2020). Moreover, there is a complex coupling relationship among different AM-induced factors, which collectively influence the anisotropy of AM alloys (Dai et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024a). Without quantifying the contributions of each LPBF-induced factor, the mechanism for the temperature-dependent anisotropy in LPBF superalloys remains an unresolved issue. One critical gap is the lack of comprehensive tests for LPBF superalloys to clarify the evolution of anisotropic behaviour from RT to ETs. Another gap is the lack of dislocation evolution history, which is crucial for fundamentally understanding the anisotropic behaviour of LPBF superalloys from RT to ETs. In this regard, micro-mechanics simulations serve as a powerful tool to bridge this gap.

In this work, a superalloy with representative microstructures is fabricated using LPBF technology. An anomalous anisotropic behaviour is revealed through tensile tests conducted on as-built (AB) and heat-treated (HT) specimens from RT to ETs. Then, a dislocation-based crystal plasticity finite element (CPFE) model is developed to elucidate the underlying mechanisms of the anomalous anisotropy and quantify the contributions of each AM-induced factor. Driven by the strain gradient resulting from periodic columnar-to-crescent grains, the effect of geometrically necessary dislocation (GND) evolution on tensile behaviour is unveiled. Finally, the threshold temperature for the transition of anisotropy is predicted using CPFE simulations, which is validated against experiments. This study contributes to understanding and predicting the anisotropic behaviour of AM solid-solution-strengthened superalloys across RT to ETs, and also inspires the regulation of anisotropy at varying temperatures by tuning the microstructures.

### 2. Material and methods

#### 2.1. LPBF fabrication

The spherical gas-atomised SD3230 Ni-based superalloy powder used in this study is produced by Avimetal AM Technology (Beijing, China). The powder’s elemental composition is 21.49 Cr - 13.13 W - 2.22 Mo - 1.57 Fe - 1.30 Co - 0.46 Al - 0.34 Mn - 0.30 Si - 0.029 La - 0.48 C - Bal Ni (weight percent, wt%). As shown in Fig. 1 b, the powder exhibits notable sphericity, with a nominal powder size ranging from 15 to 53 µm and D50 of 30.42 µm. A Concept Laser Mlab 200R (GE, Germany) LPBF machine equipped with a 200 W fibre laser source (wavelength = 1070 nm) is employed to print the superalloy. A zig-zag scanning pattern is applied, along with 90° rotation in the laser path between layers (Fig. 1 d). All specimens are fabricated under optimised printing parameters (Table 1), resulting in a high density exceeding 99.5 % (measured by the Archimedes method), without any microcracks or lack of fusion defects being found in the AB state. Moreover, as demonstrated in Appendix D, the crack initiation at LPBF-induced defects is not dominant.

Fig. 1 e illustrates the mechanism of LPBF-induced periodic columnar-to-crescent grains, with formation mechanisms similar to those in previous studies (Gokcekaya et al., 2021; Lyu and Zou, 2025; Sun et al., 2019). The rapid solidification and thermal gradients during the laser scanning process, lead to the continuous growth of large columnar grains at the regions without the overlap of adjacent melt tracks. Meanwhile, due to the remelting at the overlap region, crescent grains form around the large columnar grain during the LPBF process. Fig. 1 a shows the sampling arrangement for the tensile specimens which are extracted from printed bulks with a size of  $ 70 \times 2.5 \times 20 \, mm^3 $. Specimens for vertical and horizontal tension are fabricated to investigate the anisotropy of tensile behaviour for the LPBF superalloy from RT to ETs. Moreover, the solid-solution heat treatment is conducted at  $ 1150\,^\circ C $ for 2 h (based on the ASTM-B572 standard) to eliminate the LPBF-induced residual stresses and initial dislocations (ASTM, 2016), without altering the grain morphology (demonstrated in subsequent Section 3.2.1). Since the alloy investigated in this study is a solid-solution-strengthened Ni-based superalloy with a low Al content (0.46 wt.%), the formation of  $ \gamma' $ precipitates (Ni3Al) is absent during thermal exposure. Consequently, aging treatment is not applied, as it would not contribute to additional strengthening (Adapa et al., 2025).

#### 2.3. Microstructure characterisation

The XOY and XOZ planes of the specimens are ground and polished to examine the microstructure features. The specimens are initially ground using SiC sandpapers of 320, 600, 1500, and 2500 grit, followed by sequential polishing with diamond suspensions of 3 μm and 1 μm. Finally, to enhance the quality of electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) and X-ray diffraction (XRD) data acquisition, the specimens are polished using a 0.04 μm SiO₂ suspension for 50 min. XRD characterisation is performed using a SmartLab 3 kW diffractometer (Rigaku, Japan) with Cu Kα radiation. The polished surfaces serve as the XRD test surfaces, with measurements conducted over a 2θ range of 40–100° at a scanning speed of 3°/min. EBSD analysis is conducted under a JSM-7800F (JEOL, Japan) field emission scanning electron microscope, combined with a NordlysMax³ (Oxford Instructions, Oxford, UK) detector at a 20 kV acceleration voltage and a probe current of grade 16. The EBSD scanning area is 800 × 800 μm², with a step size of 1 μm. The EBSD data are post-processed by ATEX software (Beausir and Fundenberger, 2017), and our in-house code (Guo et al., 2025). Specimens near the fracture surfaces are cut into disc-shaped transmission electron microscope (TEM) samples using electrical discharge machining. These disc-shaped specimens are then mechanically thinned to 50 μm. Thinning is further performed using a PIPS II 695 precision ion polishing system (Gatan, USA), starting at 5 keV, with ion gun angles of ±8° until perforation occurs. Subsequently, the ion beam energy is gradually reduced to 4.5 keV at ±6° for 5 min, 4.0 keV at ±4° for 5 min, and finally 3 keV at ±3° for 5 min. The microstructure is observed using an FEI Talos F200X (Thermo Fisher, USA) field emission TEM with an acceleration voltage of 200 kV.

The algorithm mapping GND density based on EBSD data is mainly based on our previous work (Guo et al., 2025), with a brief introduction provided below. The local lattice curvature tensor  $ \kappa $ is utilized to calculate the Nye tensor  $ \alpha $:

 $$ \begin{aligned}\alpha_{ik}&=\kappa_{ki}-\delta_{ki}\kappa_{mm}\\\kappa_{ij}&=\frac{\partial\phi_{i}}{\partial\boldsymbol{x}_{j}}\end{aligned} $$ 

where  $ \phi_{i} $ is the misorientation angle regarding i axis, x is the position vector,  $ \delta_{ki} $ is the Kronecker symbol. The slip system-resolved GND densities can be linked with the Nye tensor:

 $$ \alpha=A\rho_{\mathrm{G N D}} $$ 

There are 9 constraints provided in Eq. (2), but we have 18 independent variables to be solved in  $ \rho_{\mathrm{GND}} $ for FCC metals (Pantleon, 2008). Thus, we assume that the GND densities exist in the configuration with the lowest total energy, and apply the L1 minimization algorithm to estimate the GND density at each slip system (Zhu et al., 2016):

 $$ \sum_{n=1}^{18}w^{n}\rho_{\mathrm{GND}}^{n}=\min $$ 

where  $ w^{n} $ (1 for edge GNDs,  $ (1-\mu) $ for screw GNDs) is the weight associated with the line energy of dislocations, and n is the GND type.

There are two main limitations in the EBSD-based algorithm for resolving GND density. First, only 5 components in the Nye tensor are accessible when using EBSD data as an input (Pantleon, 2008), while the Nye tensor contains 9 components in total. Secondly, due to the lack of sufficient constraints for slip system-dependent GNDs, the L1 minimization algorithm is used to approximate the GND densities, which could also introduce some errors in the resolved GND densities due to the inadequate constraints. To overcome these limitations, 3D EBSD techniques could be employed in future studies.

Moreover, the EBSD data are utilized to map the residual deformation gradient and plastic strians. First, the Nye tensor  $ \alpha $ is linked with  $ F_{p0} $ (Acharya and Bassani, 2000):

 $$ \boldsymbol{\alpha}=\left(\nabla\times\mathbf{F_{p0}}\right)^{\mathrm{T}} $$ 

 $ F_{p0} $ is estimated through the pseudo-inverse method (Penrose, 1955) based on the  $ \alpha $ derived from EBSD results, with the detailed procedure and validation in our previous work (Hu et al., 2024a). The resolved initial  $ F_{p0} $ in AB state induced by the thermal stresses during LPBF process, is employed as the initial plastic deformation gradients in CPFE simulations as the pre-condition, to consider the effect of residual stresses. Additionally, this method is also used to capture the residual plastic strain induced by tensile deformation, in order to validate the simulated strain partitioning behaviour. The residual  $ F_{p} $ resolved using EBSD can be converted into residual plastic strains through the Green–Lagrange formation (Dill, 2006).

#### 2.4. Dislocation-based damage-coupled crystal plasticity model

CPFE modeling is employed to understand the anisotropy of LPBF superalloy at RT and ET. The LPBF-induced initial dislocations and residual deformation are incorporated in the simulations, which are extracted from EBSD data, with detailed procedures in our previous work (Hu et al., 2024a). The GNDs resolved at individual slip systems are introduced to capture the strain gradient effect arising from columnar-to-crescent grains. Additionally, the stored energy density (SED) (Wan et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2024), is integrated into the CPFE simulations as an indicator to display damage distributions. The details of the crystal plasticity model are presented below.

The deformation gradient F can be decomposed into elastic part  $ F_{e} $ and plastic part  $ F_{p} $:

 $$ \mathbf{F}=\mathbf{F_{e}}\mathbf{F_{p}} $$ 

in which the residual deformation  $ F_{p0} $ formed in the LPBF process is taken as the initial condition of the plastic deformation gradient, to capture the effects of residual stresses. The Green–Lagrange elastic strain tensor is written as (Lu et al., 2020; Pedersen, 2005):

 $$ \varepsilon_{\mathrm{e}}={\frac{1}{2}}\left(\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{e}}^{\mathrm{T}}\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{e}}-\mathbf{I}\right) $$ 

Then, the stress tensor is calculated as follows (Addessio et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020):

 $$ \sigma=\mathbf{C}:\varepsilon_{\mathrm{e}} $$ 

where C is the elasticity tensor. The shear strain rates  $ \dot{\gamma}^{s} $ across all slip systems collectively contribute to the plastic strain rate (Liu et al., 2025; Wong et al., 2016):

 $$ \dot{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}_{\mathbf{p}}=\dot{\mathbf{F}}_{\mathbf{p}}\mathbf{F}_{\mathbf{p}}^{-1}=\sum_{s=1}^{N_{\mathrm{slip}}}(\mathbf{s}^{s}\otimes\mathbf{n}^{s})\dot{\gamma}^{s} $$ 

where  $ N_{slip} $ is the number of slip systems,  $ s^{s} $ is the unit vector along the slip direction, and  $ n^{s} $ is the unit vector normal to the slip plane.

The plastic now rule proposed by Peirce et al. is employed to calculate the slip rate in each slip system (Peirce et al., 1983; Rice, 1971; Xu et al., 2025):

 $$ \dot{\gamma}^{s}=\dot{\gamma}_{ref}\left(\frac{\left|\tau^{s}\right|}{\tau_{c}^{s}(1-D)}\right)^{n}sign(\tau^{s}) $$ 

where  $ \tau_{c}^{s} $ is known as the total slip resistance of the slip system (Guo et al., 2024), n is the exponent of the power law, and  $ \dot{\gamma}_{\mathrm{ref}} $ is the reference slip rate.

Under the framework of continuum damage mechanics, the damage variable $D$ is incorporated in the flow rule, to predict the crack nucleation, which represents the proportion of reduction in the effective bearing area. The damage variable results from various microdefects, such as atomic vacancies and micro-pores formed during the deformation, particularly in ET conditions (Chaboche, 1988; Guo et al., 2023). At the moment of $D=1$, the fraction of micro-defects is high enough to result in macroscopic crack nucleation (Lemaître, 1985). Since the damage variable leads to reductions in effective bearing area and stiffness, the $r^{s}$ is replaced by the effective resolved shear stress $\tau^{s}/(1-D)$ in Eq. (9). The damage $D$ will be derived from SED subsequently.

The  $ \tau_{c}^{s} $ in Eq. (9) is written as (Bayley et al., 2006; Zhang et al., 2023b):

 $$ \tau_{\mathrm{c}}^{s}=\tau_{0}^{s}+\varphi_{0}G b\sqrt{\sum_{\alpha=1}^{N_{\mathrm{slip}}}\chi_{\alpha}^{s}\left(\left|\rho_{\mathrm{S S D}}^{\alpha}\right|+\left|\rho_{\mathrm{G N D e}}^{\alpha}\right|+\left|\rho_{\mathrm{G N D s}}^{\alpha}\right|\right)} $$ 

where  $ \rho_{\text{GNDe}}^{\alpha} $ and  $ \rho_{\text{GNDs}}^{\alpha} $ are the edge and screw components of GND density,  $ \rho_{\text{SSD}}^{\alpha} $ is the immobile SSD density,  $ \varphi_0 $ is the pre-factor of the Taylor hardening law and  $ \chi_{\alpha}^s $ is the latent hardening matrix, and  $ \tau_0^s $ is the initial slip resistance capturing the lattice friction and solid-solution strengthening in the LPBF superalloy. The evolution of statistically stored dislocation (SSD) density  $ \rho_{\text{SSD}}^{\alpha} $ is given as (Hu et al., 2024a):

 $$ \dot{\rho}_{\mathrm{SSD}}^{s}=\left(K\sqrt{\left|\rho_{\mathrm{SSD}}^{s}\right|+\left|\rho_{\mathrm{GNDe}}^{s}\right|+\left|\rho_{\mathrm{GNDs}}^{s}\right|}-2y_{\mathrm{c}}\left|\rho_{\mathrm{SSD}}^{s}\right|\right)\frac{\left|\dot{\gamma}^{s}\right|}{b} $$ 

where  $ \rho_{GNDe}^{s} $ and  $ \rho_{GNDs}^{s} $ are the edge and screw GND densities,  $ \dot{\gamma}^{s} $ is the resolved shear strain rate, K is a coefficient representing the accumulation rate of SSD. The  $ y_{c} $ is the critical annihilation distance governing the dislocation annihilation rate. Additionally, the LPBF-induced initial SSD density is uniformly distributed within the simulation domain. Moreover, the GNDs are incorporated to consider the extra strengthening effect induced by strain gradients (Arsenlis and Parks, 1999):

 $$ \left\{\begin{aligned}\rho_{\mathrm{GNDe}}^{s}&=-\frac{1}{b}\nabla\gamma^{s}\cdot\mathbf{s}^{s}\\ \rho_{\mathrm{GNDs}}^{s}&=\quad\frac{1}{b}\nabla\gamma^{s}\cdot\mathbf{t}^{s}\end{aligned}\right. $$ 

where  $ \mathbf{t}^s = \mathbf{s}^s \times \mathbf{n}^s $ is the direction of screw dislocation motion. When implementing Eq. (12), the slip system-dependent accumulated slip  $ r^s $ is calculated by integrating the slip increments provided by Eq. (9). Subsequently, the edge and screw GND densities are resolved onto each slip system based on the gradients of accumulated slip, according to the original definition of GNDs in Eq. (12). The scalar GND and SSD densities defined in Eq. (15) are used to calculate SED.

The SED G is employed as the damage indicator, which represents the internal energy stored around the dislocation structures (Wan et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2024):

 $$ G=\int\frac{\xi\boldsymbol{\sigma}:\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_{\mathrm{p}}}{\sqrt{\rho_{\mathrm{GND}}+\rho_{\mathrm{SSD}}} $$ 

where  $ \xi $ is a constant that is given as 0.05. The micro-cracks are likely to nucleate at the locations with higher SED, then the scalar damage indicator ranging from 0 to 1 is defined as:

 $$ D=\frac{G}{G_{cr}} $$ 

where  $ G_{cr} $ is the critical SED for crack nucleation.

Moreover, the CPFE simulations are performed under the open-source MOOSE framework (Permann et al., 2020). This platform facilitates solving the GND densities induced by strain gradients, by using the embedded solver for spatial gradients. The stress-based Newton-Raphson iterative solving approach is employed, offering greater computational efficiency. The parameters are calibrated in Appendix A, with details of parameter determination provided in Table A.1. ### 3. Results

### 3.1. Anomalous anisotropic tensile behaviours from RT to ET

Fig. 2 illustrates the tensile properties of AB and HT specimens under RT and ET conditions. The average values of yield strength and total elongation are reported along with their corresponding standard deviations, based on the two specimens tested under each condition. At the RT (Fig. 2 a), the AB specimens show higher strength than the HT specimens, with pronounced anisotropy between the horizontal and vertical tensions. #### 3.2. Microstructures and simulation results

##### 3.2.1. Microstructures

XRD analysis shows that both the AB (Fig. 3 a1) and HT (Fig. 3 b1) specimens exhibit typical face-centred cubic (FCC) austenite microstructures. As illustrated in Fig. 3 a1, the (200) diffraction peak appears as the main peak in the AB-XOY and AB-XOZ diffraction patterns. During solidification, the FCC grains tend to grow preferentially along the thermal gradient in the <001> direction, resulting in a pronounced (200) diffraction peak (Kurz et al., 2023). The (200) peak intensity on the XOZ plane is higher than that on the XOY plane (Fig. 3 a1), indicating a stronger temperature gradient-induced crystallographic texture along the BD compared to the OX or OZ direction. It is worth noting that XRD characterizes the crystallographic orientation normal to the observation planes. This observation is also consistent with a previous study using similar printing parameters (Yin et al., 2022). As shown in Fig. 3 b1, the XRD patterns of the HT specimens remain roughly unchanged compared with those of AB specimens. The XRD patterns indicate that the heat treatment has minimal effect on the orientation of the texture.

As shown in the EBSD orientation map (Fig. 3 a2), the LPBF-induced large columnar grains exhibit [001] preferred orientation along BD, surrounded by crescent grains with random orientations. Fig. 3 b2 indicates that the features of grain orientations remain unchanged after the heat treatment, which similarly exhibits the [001] preferred orientation along BD. This is consistent with the XRD results. TEM characterisation further confirms that the primary difference in microstructures before and after heat treatment lies in the dislocation density. As shown in Fig. 3 a3 and b3, the AB specimen exhibits a dense dislocation network around the sub-grain boundaries, while the dislocation density is greatly reduced after heat treatment. The additional TEM observation provided in Appendix E further corroborates this trend. These results are consistent with the microstructure evolution of LPBF HX superalloy before and after heat treatment in literature (Sun et al., 2021). Previous studies demonstrated that the high initial dislocation density is caused by the large temperature gradient and thermal stresses in the rapid solidification process of LPBF (Hu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2020).

The aspect ratio distribution (Fig. 3 c) of grains indicates that the grain morphology within the YOZ plane for the AB and HT specimens presents a similar periodic pattern of columnar-to-crescent grains. The aspect ratios of the columnar grains mostly exceed 5, with those of crescent grains close to 1. Moreover, all the grains observed from the XOZ plane show aspect ratios close to 1. The histograms of grain aspect ratio shown in Fig. 3 d–e are utilized to quantify the effect of heat treatment on grain morphology. As shown in Fig. 3 d, AB and HT specimens similarly contain 89 % and 83 % of large columnar grains within XOY planes, featuring aspect ratios greater than 2. Besides, Fig. 3 e further demonstrates that when viewed from the XOZ plane, the aspect ratios for all grains are close to 1, without the feature of large columnar grains. Those experimental observations are consistent with the expectation in Fig. 1 e. Within the XOZ plane, AB and HT specimens show comparable proportions of crescent grains with aspect ratios less than 2. Thus, the quantitative comparisons between the AB and HT states indicate that heat treatment has minimal influence on grain morphology. Statistically, within the XOZ plane, the average grain size is 8.2 μm for the AB state and 8.8 μm after heat treatment. Besides, within the XOY plane, the average grain sizes are 14.5 μm (AB) and 13.5 μm (HT). Hence, the effect of heat treatment on average grain size is less than 10 %, possibly because the heat treatment temperature is insufficient to promote grain growth.

##### 3.2.2. Representative volume element

To reflect the distinctive columnar-to-crescent grain distribution in LPBF superalloys, a representative volume element (RVE) is constructed within a domain of 330 μm × 330 μm. As shown in Fig. 4 a-b, the RVE is selected as a single periodic structure for columnar-to-crescent grains, including a single columnar grain and surrounding crescent grains. As demonstrated in Fig. 4 c, the grain size distributions of simplified RVE and original EBSD are similar in statistics, suggesting that the selected RVE is representative. Moreover, as depicted in Appendix B, the simplified RVE closely resembles the orientation distribution of the original EBSD data. It should be noted that the same RVE is used for both AB and HT specimens, since no significant changes in grain orientation and morphology occur during the heat treatment, as demonstrated in Section 3.2.1. As illustrated in Fig. 4 e, for the finite element model with 300 × 300 hexahedral elements, uniform displacement is applied on the BC2 under horizontal tension, and BC1 under vertical tension, respectively, while the BC4 and BC3 opposite to the loaded ones are fixed in normal direction as constraints in each case.

By combining Fig. 4 b and d, the grain size mapped with crystal orientations indicates that the large columnar grain shows [001] preferred orientation along the BD. In contrast, the crescent grains show randomly distributed crystal orientations. To identify the soft and hard zones within the RVE, horizontal or vertical tension is applied to each element, and the stress-strain response of each element is obtained based on local single-crystal CPFE simulation, without running the finite-element computation for the whole RVE. Taking 1 % plastic strain as the criterion of yielding for each element, the local yield strength is mapped in Fig. 4 f and g, indicating that the columnar grains are ‘softer’ than crescent grains under both horizontal and vertical tensions. Similarly, this trend resembles the uniaxial tensile results of FCC single-crystals, where [100] and [111] orientations exhibit the lowest and highest yield strength respectively, with [110] falling between the two (Gupta and Bronkhorst, 2021; Reed, 2008). Therefore, under both horizontal and vertical tensions, the large columnar grains and crescent grains can be regarded as the ‘soft’ and ‘harder’ zones, respectively.

#### 3.2.3. Strain, stress and GND distributions

As listed in Table S1, the major difference between the simulations at RT and ET is that, more pronounced dislocation annihilation is incorporated at ET, in the evolution equation of  $ \rho_{\mathrm{SSD}}^{\prime} $ (Eq. (11)). In CPFE simulations, increasing  $ y_{c} $ is the only approach to reproduce the strain softening at ETs, which is observed in experimental stress-strain responses (Fig. 2 c). Physically, the dislocation annihilation manifested as the dislocation climb or diffusion, etc., is highly dependent on temperatures (Galindo-Nava and Rivera-Díaz-del-Castillo, 2012). As a thermally activated process, the dislocation annihilation rate is amplified at ETs (Huang et al., 2009), resulting in a higher  $ y_{c} $ value.

### 4. Discussion

### 4.1. Mechanism for anomalous anisotropy from RT to ET

The analysis on the number of activated slip systems is utilized to interpret the anisotropy at RT. As shown in Fig. 8 a-b, both horizontal and vertical tension show more activated slip systems within the columnar grain, attributed to the lower local yield strength (Fig. 4 f-g). Under the same global strain, a smaller number of activated slip systems suggest less complete plastic deformation, which in turn contributes to higher strength. Based on the statistical results shown in Fig. 8 c, vertical tension exhibits a higher average number of activated slip systems within the RVE, along with a more dispersed distribution as indicated by a higher standard deviation. Hence, the more complete slip activation under vertical tension results in lower strength at RT.

The transition of anisotropic behaviour at ETs is understood from the perspective of dislocation strengthening. Compared with the cases of RT with strain hardening, enhanced dislocation annihilation is essential to capture the strain softening at ETs for both AB and HT specimens (Fig. A.1), which could be the primary contributor of anomalous anisotropy at ETs. The temperature dependence of dislocation annihilation will be elaborated in Section 4.4. On the other hand, as depicted in the subsequent GND analysis in Section 4.3 (Fig. 14 a-b), the gradient of grain size-induced GNDs account for less than 10 % of the total dislocation density at the early stage of deformation under both RT and 900 °C. Moreover, from the stress-strain responses in the cases with and without GNDs (Fig. 14 d-e), the GND densities have a neglectable effect on the strength and strain hardening rate at 2 % global strain. Each evidence demonstrates that SSDs dominate the dislocation strengthening at the early stage of deformation.

Hence, the incremental scalar SSD induced by applied deformation, is chosen to elaborate the mechanism of temperature-dependent anisotropic behaviour. In Section 4.4, the Arrhenius-type formula is utilized to capture the temperature dependence of critical annihilation distance  $ y_{c} $. Despite LPBF-induced initial SSDs, the incremental SSD densities are solely caused by the applied deformation during the tension of specimens, which are derived from Eq. (11). As the initial SSDs are assumed to be uniformly distributed in CPFE modeling, the incremental SSDs can represent the distribution of total SSDs. The scalar SSD is defined in Eq. (15), which is calculated using the SSDs resolved at individual slip systems.

Moreover, it is necessary to elaborate on the initial SSD and GND densities induced by the LPBF process. The thermal gradient during the LPBF process produces large thermal stresses, which locally causes plastic deformation and the formation of corresponding SSDs (Eq. (11)). Meanwhile, due to the non-uniform distribution of thermal stresses, the GNDs are incorporated to accommodate the gradient of plastic deformation (Eq. (12)). Hence, both SSDs and GNDs co-exist in the AB states. Furthermore, the initial GND densities are directly extracted from EBSD data, while the uniformly distributed initial SSDs are calibrated by comparing the yield strength of AB and HT specimens.

As shown in Fig. 9 a, at the RT with smaller dislocation annihilation, the incremental scalar SSD is concentrated at columnar grain under horizontal tension, but exhibits uniform distribution under vertical tension. According to Eq. (11), when the  $ y_c $ is minimal at RT, the slip-induced dislocation accumulation produces SSD patterns that well resemble the strain distributions exhibited in Fig. 5 a–b. Therefore, the dislocation annihilation leads to a more significant reduction in dislocation strengthening within the columnar grain under horizontal tension, resulting in a lower YS under horizontal tension compared with the case under vertical tension at ET.

The TEM characterization is utilized to validate the simulated dislocation patterns exhibited in Fig. 9. As shown in Fig. 10, compared with the initial dislocation density at AB state (Fig. 3 a3), the applied deformation increases the dislocation density at RT, but reduces the dislocation density at ET within both columnar and crescent grain regions, which is consistent with the trends in simulations. Moreover, under the horizontal tension at RT, the dislocation density in large columnar grain is higher than that in crescent grains (Fig. 10 a and c), whereas the large columnar grain shows lower dislocation density compared with the crescent grains at ET (Fig. 10 b and d). Those TEM observations align with the simulated dislocation density patterns.

As demonstrated in Section 3.1, with the temperature increasing, the EL under horizontal tension tends to be lower than that under vertical tension. The SED as a widely recognized damage indicator for predicting the microcrack nucleation, is employed to understand this trend. As shown in Fig. 11, under both RT and ET, the horizontal tension shows more pronounced SED localization at the columnar grain. Furthermore, by comparing Fig. 11 a1 with b1, the SED localization within the columnar grain is more significant at ET. As shown in Eq. (13), the SED is derived from plastic dissipative energy that is calculated using stress and plastic strain tensors. Under horizontal tension, the average stress within columnar grains is comparable with that in crescent grains to maintain the force balance. Hence, without the deformation coordination, the dislocation annihilation-assisted strain softening within columnar grain leads to more significant SED or damage localization under the horizontal tension at ET. As a result, compared with vertical tension, the horizontal one exhibits lower EL at higher temperatures with enhanced dislocation annihilation.

### 4.2. Mechanism for relaxation of strength anisotropy after heat treatment

The CPFE simulations are further employed to understand the mechanisms for the relaxation of strength anisotropy after heat treatment. As demonstrated in Fig. 3, the HT process can eliminate LPBF-induced initial GNDs, initial SSDs, and residual deformation ( $ F_{PO} $), without altering the grain orientations and morphology. Based on the algorithm described in Section 2.4, the initial GND and residual deformation obtained from EBSD are shown in Fig. 13 d-e. The initial GNDs localized at grain boundaries introduce anisotropic initial dislocation strengthening, which more significantly enhances the YS for horizontal tension at RT, compared with vertical tension (Toursangsaraki et al., 2024). Meanwhile, the LPBF-induced residual strains sharing a similar inhomogeneous pattern with initial GNDs, also contribute to enhancing the anisotropy. Moreover, the initial SSD with uniform distribution in CPFE simulations amplifies the intrinsic texture-induced anisotropy for columnar-to-crescent grains (Herath et al., 2024). In Fig. 13 a, regarding the anisotropy at RT, the initial GND, SSD, and residual deformation are individually incorporated into the CPFE simulations, with the HT state as the baseline. As marked in Fig. 13 a, the anisotropy indicator  $ \Delta $ is defined as the strength difference between horizontal tension and vertical tension. As quantified in Fig. 13 b, the contributions to the anisotropy enhancement from HT to AB followed the order of residual deformation > initial SSD > initial GND at the RT condition.

At the ET condition, the contributions of LPBF-induced factors on anisotropy enhancement are shown in Fig. 13 c. The residual deformation and initial GND tend to increase the anisotropy, resembling the behaviour observed at RT. In contrast to the trend at RT, the anisotropy at ET is significantly reduced because of the initial SSD. As demonstrated in Fig. 9, the enhanced dislocation annihilation at the columnar grains induces the transition in anisotropy behaviour at ETs, under the prerequisite of enabled initial SSD. ### 4.4. Threshold temperature for the anisotropy transition

The CPFE model is further used to determine the threshold temperature for the occurrence of anisotropy transition. As previously demonstrated, the dislocation annihilation mechanism dominates the anomalous anisotropy. Since the dislocation annihilation is a thermally activated process (Huang et al., 2009), the parameter  $ y_c $ governing the dislocation annihilation rate in Eq. (11), could monotonically increase with temperature. As schematized in Fig. 15 a, the normal (horizontal strength > vertical strength) and anomalous (vertical strength > horizontal strength) anisotropy occurs along with the strain hardening (high  $ y_c $) and strain softening (low  $ y_c $) at RT and ET, respectively. Hence, there should be a threshold value of  $ y_c $, at which the horizontal and vertical tensions have the same strength with the absence of strain hardening or softening. Since SSDs mainly contribute to the strengthening at the early stage of deformation, regarding the  $ y_c $ below and beyond the threshold at RT (strain hardening) and 900 °C (strain softening), the overall SSD density tends to increase and decrease with slip accumulation, respectively. ### 5. Conclusions

In this paper, an anomalous anisotropic behaviour from RT to ETs in a solid-solution-strengthened superalloy LPBF superalloy featuring columnar-to-crescent grains is experimentally revealed, with the underlying mechanisms elucidated through CPFE

simulations. The main conclusions of this study can be summarised as follows.

1. Compared with vertical tension, horizontal tension shows YS higher at RT, but exhibits lower YS at ET (900 °C). The transition of anisotropy from RT to ET is governed by the temperature-dependent dislocation annihilation within the columnar grains, with the threshold temperature predicted to be 600 °C.

2. Compared with HT state, the enhancement of anisotropy in AB state is attributed to several LPBF-induced factors, ranked in order of impact: residual deformation > initial SSD > initial GND at RT, and initial SSD > initial GND > residual deformation at ET.

3. Damage is localized within large columnar grains under horizontal tension, resulting in lower EL compared to vertical tension in both the AB and HT states. This effect becomes more pronounced at ETs due to enhanced damage localization facilitated by dislocation annihilation.

4. The strain gradient provides a more significant strengthening effect at ETs owing to a high GND ratio, particularly in horizontal tension, where the higher GND density arises from columnar-to-crescent grain structures.

This paper presents an integrated work for understanding and predicting the anisotropic behaviour of AM superalloys. In the future study, the grain morphology could be tailored using AM technique, to achieve the tunable temperature-dependent anisotropy adapted to various applications.
